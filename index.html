01 — Overview

Program Learning Outcomes

The M.S. in Applied Data Science program is built around six learning outcomes. Below is each outcome and a description of what it means.

PLO 01

Collect, Store & Access Data

Collect, store, and access data by identifying and leveraging applicable technologies. This means working with databases, cloud storage, APIs, and other tools depending on the data format and scale.

PLO 02

Create Actionable Insight

Create actionable insight across a range of contexts (societal, business, political) using data and the full data science life cycle. The goal is to produce something useful that helps people make real decisions.

PLO 03

Visualization & Predictive Models

Apply visualization and predictive models to help generate actionable insight. Visualizations make patterns easier to find, and predictive models let you draw conclusions while accounting for multiple variables.

PLO 04

Programming in R & Python

Use programming languages such as R and Python to support the generation of actionable insight. These are the primary languages in data science, essential for cleaning, analyzing, and modeling data.

PLO 05

Communicate Insights

Communicate insights gained via visualization and analytics to a broad range of audiences, including project sponsors and technical team leads. Analysis only matters if you can convey it clearly.

PLO 06

Ethics in Data Science

Apply ethics in the development, use, and evaluation of data and predictive models, covering fairness, bias, transparency, and privacy. Improper use of data science has real consequences.

02 — Projects

Project Descriptions

Below are detailed descriptions of each project, including the problem, my contributions, the findings, and the learning outcomes each project addressed.

eSC Energy Demand Forecasting and Optimization

IST 687 · Group Project

The goal of this project was to help eSC, a hypothetical electric utility provider in South Carolina and parts of North Carolina, anticipate and manage energy demand during peak summer months. Rather than recommending costly infrastructure expansions, we focused on identifying the key drivers of energy consumption and providing customers with actionable ways to reduce their usage during high-demand periods.

The project used CSV data stored in AWS S3 buckets, including building-level attributes, hourly energy consumption records, and county-level weather data. R was used throughout for data cleaning, exploratory analysis, visualization, and modeling. Three machine learning models were trained and evaluated: Linear Regression, CART Tree, and XGBoost. XGBoost was selected as the final model based on its performance, achieving a mean absolute error of 0.36 kWh on the test set.

My contribution was primarily data cleaning and processing. I reduced the building dataset from 171 columns to a usable set of 53 by removing zero-variance columns, reviewing and dropping uninformative features, and splitting combined columns into separate fields. I also handled the joining of all three datasets, merging building attributes, hourly energy records for all 5,710 buildings, and county-level weather data into a single dataframe of over four million rows ready for analysis.

The analysis found that homes with lower hot water usage, solar panels, finished basements, and fuel oil or propane heating systems consistently showed lower energy consumption. By educating customers on these factors, eSC could reduce peak demand without investing in new infrastructure, saving customers money on their energy bills in the process.

R XGBoost CART AWS S3 Data Wrangling

Effect of Air and Water Quality on Crop Production in India

IST 707 · Group Project

This project set out to understand how air and water quality affect rice production across India, and to give institutions like State Pollution Control Boards and agricultural extension services quantitative evidence they could use to make policy decisions.

We pulled four datasets from Kaggle and two from the ICRISAT data portal, covering air quality, water quality, crop production, irrigation, rainfall, and farm size across Indian states and districts spanning several decades. Python was used for all data cleaning, aggregation, joining, and modeling.

I contributed in two main areas. On the data side, I handled most of the cleaning and joining of the six datasets into a single usable table. This was tricky because each dataset was sampled at a different geographic and temporal level, so I had to aggregate everything to the yearly district level, use fuzzy string matching to resolve spelling inconsistencies in state and district names, and apply hierarchical imputation to deal with significant missing data across several key columns. I also built the hierarchical mixed effects model used for statistical inference. The model accounted for the nested structure of the data by grouping at the state level with district-level random slopes on year, producing more accurate coefficient estimates and p-values than a standard regression would have.

The analysis found that AQI, water pH, and water conductivity all had statistically significant negative effects on rice production, even after controlling for rainfall and farm size. This gives pollution control boards a concrete agricultural justification for tightening emissions and water quality standards beyond just health outcomes.

Drugs in Powerlifting: Worth It?

IST 719 · Individual Project

The goal of this project was to use powerlifting competition data to examine whether performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs) provide a meaningful competitive advantage, and to present the findings in a way that athletes, coaches, and casual gym-goers could understand and act on.

The data came from OpenPowerlifting.org and included 3.6 million rows of competition results since 1995. I narrowed the analysis to the USPA federation, which is the only federation with both tested and untested divisions, then filtered to lifters using no additional equipment and used their best meet performance. All analysis and visualization was done in R using the tidyverse and ggrepel packages, and the final deliverable was a designed poster.

The main finding was that PEDs provide surprisingly little advantage at most levels of competition. The median squat and deadlift differences between tested and untested divisions were small, and the bench press difference was only about 16.5 lbs. The gap becomes more noticeable at the elite end, particularly above 200 lbs bodyweight, where the untested group pulls ahead more clearly. The practical takeaway for most athletes is that PED use is unlikely to change where they place in a competition, and the risk-reward ratio does not make sense unless competing at a world-class level.

04 — Blog Post

Reflections on the Program

What I Expected to Learn

When I started the M.S. in Applied Data Science at Syracuse, I'd finished a B.S. in Data Science and had a decent foundation in programming, statistics, and machine learning. I knew how to write Python and R, I'd built models, and I'd done enough coursework to get comfortable with the basics, but I also knew I had significant weaknesses. I was bad at communicating my work; I'd get deep into a project and struggle to explain it to someone who wasn't already familiar with the data and the methods. I also wanted more experience with the full data science life cycle, from collecting messy real-world data to producing something a business or organization could actually use. I wasn't just looking for more technical knowledge, I wanted to become more well-rounded, which is especially important now with modern LLMs.

I also came into the program hoping to get better at visualization. In undergrad I could make charts and graphs, but I didn't think carefully about whether they told a clear story or whether my audience could easily read them. I tended to overload my visuals with too much information, too much chart junk, hard to read text, and many other problems. I wanted to patch that weaknesss. On the modeling side, I was comfortable with standard regression and classification, but I hadn't worked with more advanced techniques like hierarchical models or ensemble methods on large, messy datasets. Furthermore, I expected the program to challenge me in my ability to marry those creative skills with those technical skills.

Beyond the technical stuff, I was hoping the program would give me more project experience I could talk about concretely in interviews and on my resume. Having a portfolio of real projects where I could point to specific contributions and specific results felt more valuable than just listing courses on a transcript. It's important to "show your work" so that hiring managers and recruiters can easily see your skills.

What I Learned: The Six Learning Outcomes

The program has six learning outcomes, and I hit all of them across my coursework and projects. Here's how each one played out.

I demonstrated the first learning outcome (collecting, storing, and accessing data) in every project in this portfolio. For the eSC energy forecasting project, we pulled structured CSV files from AWS S3 buckets and merged the three slarge datasets: buildings, energy, and weather. In my crop production and pollution project, we pulled data from Kaggle and ICRISAT (International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics) and managed to merge several datasets at different geographic and temporal levels. Additionally, I worked with a 3.6-million-row dataset from OpenPowerlifting.org for the powerlifting visualization project. All of these datasets required different collection methods, different ways to store and share access to the data, and different strategies for processing them into what we needed.

Creating actionable insight is the outcome that changed how I think about data science the most. It's easy to do analysis for the sake of finding something interesting, but the program pushed me to always ask: who's going to use this, and what decision will it help them make? Our primary insights for the eSC project gave customers actionable strategies for reducing energy costs. We gave statistical evidence to pollution control boards so they could justify increasing emissions standards within the crop production project. In the powerlifting project, the insight was why for athletes PEDs aren't worth the risk at most levels. These projects spanned multiple different industries and also spanned the full data science lifecycle.

Visualization and predictive modeling showed up in different ways across the three projects. The eSC project used XGBoost, CART trees, and linear regression, and we evaluated each one on the test set to choose the best performer. The crop production project used a hierarchical mixed effects model, which was the first time I'd built anything that accounted for nested data structure with random slopes. The powerlifting project was visualization-heavy, with the final deliverable being a designed poster that had to communicate the findings at a glance. The Visual Analytics track, specifically IST 719 and IST 737, is where I really leveled up on visualization. More on that below.

Both R and Python were used heavily. R was the primary tool in IST 687 and IST 719, where I used it for data wrangling, modeling, and visualization with packages like tidyverse and ggrepel. Python was the primary tool in IST 707, where I used it for cleaning, fuzzy string matching, aggregation, and building the mixed effects model. By the end of the program I felt equally comfortable in both, which wasn't the case coming in. I had a strong Python preference in undergrad and had to warm up to R.

Communication was the outcome I targeted when choosing the Visual Analytics track, and it's probably the area where I grew the most. IST 719 taught me how to think about who's looking at a visualization and whether it tells a clear story. IST 737 extended that into interactive dashboards with Tableau. Before these courses, I'd make a chart and call it done. Now I think about hierarchy, flow, and whether a non-technical person could look at my work and walk away understanding the main point.

Ethics touched every project differently. In the eSC project, we used anonymized data, avoided target leakage through stratified sampling, and chose low-bias models. In the crop production project, we had to think carefully about imputation bias, the limitations of causal claims from observational data, and the potential impact of policy recommendations on smaller farms. In the powerlifting project, I presented drug use in an unbiased, transparent light and gave a data-driven conclusion rather than a moral judgment. Ethics isn't just a box to check. It requires thinking about who's affected by the work and what could go wrong.

Three Projects That Shaped My Education

The first project that stands out is the eSC Energy Demand Forecasting project from IST 687. This was my first real group data science project in the program, and it taught me a lot about what happens before the modeling even starts. My main contribution was data cleaning and processing: reducing 171 columns down to 53, removing zero-variance and uninformative features, splitting combined columns, and then merging three separate datasets into a single dataframe of over four million rows. That merging step alone took more time and thought than I expected. It made me realize that in practice, the data preparation phase is where most of the actual work happens and is thus the most difficult section. The modeling part, comparing XGBoost, CART, and linear regression, was almost the easy part by comparison. The project also gave me my first exposure to working with cloud-stored data in S3, which is now something I list on my resume and can talk about concretely.

The second project is the crop production and pollution study from IST 707. This was probably the most technically challenging project I did in the program. The data side was difficult because I had six datasets sampled at different geographic and temporal levels, with inconsistent spelling across state and district names. I used fuzzy string matching to resolve the naming issues and hierarchical imputation to handle missing data. On the modeling side, I built a hierarchical mixed effects model, which was new to me. A standard regression would have ignored the nested structure of the data (districts within states), so I used state-level grouping with district-level random slopes on year. This gave us more reliable coefficient estimates and p-values. Finding that AQI, water pH, and water conductivity all had statistically significant negative effects on rice production gave the project a real policy implication.

The third project is the powerlifting analysis from IST 719. This one was different because it was an individual project and the final deliverable was a designed poster rather than a report or notebook. I had to take 3.6 million rows of data, filter it down to the relevant subset, do the analysis, and then present everything in a single visual format that someone could understand in a few minutes. The finding itself was interesting: PEDs provide surprisingly little advantage for most lifters. But the real learning was in the design process. I had to make hard choices about what to include and what to cut, how to arrange the visuals so they told a coherent story, and how to write concise takeaways that a non-technical audience could follow. This project is the one where the communication skills I was building in the Visual Analytics track really came together.

Internship at USAA

During the summer of 2025, I interned as a CFO Data Intern at USAA in San Antonio, Texas. The internship was part of the Chief Financial Office Data and Analytics organization, which focuses on data governance, business intelligence, insight delivery, and data science in support of USAA's financial operations. It was my first time working in a finance-oriented data role, and it gave me a perspective that coursework alone can't really provide.

My work fell into two main areas. The first was around data lineage and metadata. I automated the process of tracing the lineage of cells in Excel-based UDAs (User Defined Applications) using Python. These Excel files were used across the CFO organization for financial reporting, and understanding which cells depended on which other cells was important for auditability and governance. Before I automated it, this was a manual process. I also queried CFO report metadata from Alation using SQL and cleaned it using Python and Pandas. That metadata work fed into a broader effort I helped with: designing a new, more comprehensive CFO Report Inventory. The goal was to give the organization a single source of truth for what reports existed, what data they contained, where the data came from, and who owned them. It wasn't exciting work, but it's the kind of data management that everything else depends on.

The second area was more technical and more interesting. I built a proof-of-concept LLM chatbot using Snowflake Cortex Analyst that let users ask natural language questions about financial report data. The idea was an "Ask the Data" tool where someone in the CFO organization could type a question and get an answer pulled from the report metadata, without needing to write SQL or dig through spreadsheets themselves. I also implemented a more robust AI assistant using retrieval augmented generation (RAG) with Python, Snowflake, and Streamlit. For that project, I created embeddings of the financial reports, extracted their metadata from Alation using SQL, and loaded both structured and unstructured data into a Snowflake database. The Streamlit front end let users interact with the assistant in a clean, simple interface. Building something that other people on the team could actually demo and use was a very different experience from building a model for a homework assignment that only a professor will ever see.

The internship reinforced several things I'd been learning in the program but hadn't experienced at scale. Data governance and metadata management aren't topics that come up much in a data science curriculum, but in a large organization like USAA they're the foundation everything sits on. You can't build good models or dashboards if the underlying data isn't well documented, well governed, and well understood. I also got a much better sense of what it means to work within a regulated environment. USAA operates under strict compliance requirements, and every piece of data work has to account for internal policies, standards, and external regulations. That's a constraint I never dealt with in school.

On the technical side, the internship gave me hands-on experience with tools I'd only read about. Snowflake, Alation, and Streamlit were all new to me at the start of the summer. By the end, I was comfortable writing SQL against Snowflake, building data pipelines that moved both structured and unstructured data into cloud storage, and putting together front-end demos in Streamlit. The RAG project in particular tied together a lot of what I'd been studying in my LLM coursework. Building a RAG system for Syracuse University financial reports in IST 700 was useful preparation, but doing it in a corporate environment with real data, real users, and real compliance constraints was a different challenge entirely.

The internship also gave me experience communicating with stakeholders who aren't data scientists. Presenting to a manager who wants a clear bottom line is different from presenting to a professor who wants to see your methodology. At USAA, I had to explain what my tools did and why they mattered to people who cared about the business outcome, not the technical implementation. Knowing your audience and adjusting how you communicate is something the program talks about a lot, but the internship is where I actually had to do it for real.

Favorite Class

My favorite class was IST 719, Information Visualization. It changed how I think about my work more than any other course. Before taking it, I treated visualization as an afterthought. I'd do the analysis, make some charts, and move on. IST 719 forced me to think about visualization as its own discipline. I learned about design principles, choosing the right chart type, and making visualizations intuitively understandable. More importantly, it pushed me to think about whether a visualization tells a clear story or just displays data. That distinction sounds simple, but it's hard to execute well.

The course used R and Adobe Illustrator, which was an unexpected combination. Working in Illustrator taught me to think about layout, typography, and visual hierarchy in a way that code-based plotting doesn't. The powerlifting poster project was the capstone of that learning. I had to take a complex analysis and distill it into a single designed artifact that someone could take in within a few minutes. It was incredibly challenging, and it's the piece of work I'm most proud of from the program.

IST 737, Visual Analytic Dashboards, built on that by introducing Tableau and shifting the focus toward interactive exploration. Instead of showing a static visual, Tableau lets the user explore the data themselves. The course covered how to structure dashboards so that a user can draw their own conclusions rather than just being told what to think. Both courses together changed how I communicate and display my work. Getting the analytics right is only half the battle. The other half is making sure the findings actually land with whoever's looking at them.

Best Parts and Biggest Surprises

The best part of this program was the project experience. By the end, I had a portfolio of real work I could point to in interviews and describe concretely. I could talk ad nauseum about specific datasets, specific models, specific results, and specific contributions.

My biggest surprise was how much of real data science is not the modeling part. Coming in, I thought the hard part would be choosing the right algorithm or tuning hyperparameters. In practice, the most difficult stage is almost always data preparation: cleaning, joining, resolving inconsistencies, handling missing values, and getting everything into a format where modeling is even possible. The eSC project and the crop production project both hammered this home. I spent way more time on data wrangling than on model training in both cases.

Another surprise was how much I got out of the Visual Analytics track. I chose it because communication was a weak point, and I expected it to be a nice supplement to my technical skills. It ended up being way more impactful than that. It completely changed how I approach presenting my work, and it gave me one of the most challenging and rewarding projects in the program. I didn't expect a visualization class to push me harder than some of my machine learning courses, but it did.

That said, the program stayed at a higher level than I would've liked in some areas. Breadth is useful, but when you run into a real problem in industry or sit across from a highly technical interviewer, gaps in deep understanding become obvious fast. I also wish the program had focused more on connecting data science to business contexts and provided more practice communicating to business stakeholders and managers. Some of that is unavoidable in an academic setting. No program can fully prepare someone for production-scale data inside a real company with real constraints.

Looking Ahead

The program gave me a solid working understanding of data science. I'm not an expert in everything, but I have a clearer sense of where my gaps are and enough project experience to talk concretely about what I know and the work I've done. I want to keep pushing deeper into machine learning and statistical modeling, areas where I feel like I have a good base but not the depth I want. I'm also interested in continuing to build on my experience with large language models and generative AI, which I got exposure to through my internship and coursework but want to take further.

My goal is to find a job where I can apply what I've learned to real problems companies face. The projects in this program gave me a small taste of what that feels like, but they pale in comparison to the scale and complexity data scientists deal with in industry. However, sometimes the solutions that have the most business impact are the simplest. So I sometimes have to go against what I think is most interesting for what is most valuable. It's hard to find the right balance of technical depth and business value in many roles.

I'm not a perfect data scientist. But I know how to collect messy data, clean it, model it, visualize it, and communicate the results. I know how to think about ethics and audience and actionability. And I know where I still need to grow. I think I'm in a great spot overall coming out of a master's program.
